Chapter 3 – Analytics & Measurement

Analytics turns raw data into decisions. The goal is not “more reports” but better actions that increase profit.

3.0 – Why Analytics?

  • Digital marketing produces a lot of data, but data ≠ insight.
  • Value appears only when data → insights → changed decisions → better outcomes.
  • Analytics must connect back to the core framework: traffic, conversion, revenue per conversion.

3.1 – How Analytics Work (High-Level)

  • Analytics tools (like Google Analytics) collect data via a tracking code on pages.
  • They use cookies and events to track:
    • Who visited (basic info)
    • What they did (pageviews, clicks, events)
    • Where they came from (traffic source / medium / campaign)
    • Whether they converted (goals, e-commerce transactions)

3.2 – Metrics

Core Metrics

  • Users – unique visitors (approximate, based on cookies).
  • Sessions – visits; one user can have multiple sessions.
  • Pageviews – total pages viewed.
  • Bounce rate – % of sessions with only one pageview.
  • Conversion rate – % of sessions that complete a goal.
  • Revenue – total money from transactions.

Interpreting Metrics

  • High bounce rate on a landing page may indicate:
    • Misleading ad or keyword
    • Slow page load
    • Poor relevance or UX
  • Low conversion rate on checkout may indicate friction or wrong audience.
  • Drop in traffic could indicate SEO or ad issues.

3.3 – KPIs (Key Performance Indicators)

  • KPIs are the small set of metrics directly tied to business success.
  • Examples:
    • E-commerce: revenue, conversion rate, average order value (AOV).
    • Lead gen: number of qualified leads, cost per lead.
    • Media: pageviews per session, time on site.
  • Avoid focusing on vanity metrics – e.g., impressions or followers without connection to conversions or revenue.

3.4 – Making Better Decisions

Basic Process

  • Observe a pattern (e.g., mobile conversion is low).
  • Form hypotheses (e.g., mobile page is slow or layout is broken).
  • Test changes (A/B tests, new designs, new copy).
  • Measure impact and keep improvements.

3.5 – Attribution

  • Attribution = deciding which channels get credit for conversions.
  • Common models:
    • Last click – all credit to the final interaction.
    • First click – all credit to the first interaction.
    • Linear – equal credit to each touchpoint.
    • Time decay – more credit to recent touches.
  • All models are imperfect; platforms tend to over-claim their contribution, especially with advanced machine learning.

3.6 – Google Analytics: UA vs GA4

  • Universal Analytics (UA):
    • Session-based.
    • Emphasis on sessions, bounce rate, goals.
  • GA4:
    • Event-based (everything is an event).
    • Focus on user journeys, engagement, multiple devices.
    • Purchases tracked as events; you mark events as conversions.

Experiments & True Impact

  • Because attribution models are biased, the most reliable way to measure true impact is through experiments (A/B or regional tests).
  • Example: randomly turn ads on/off in different regions and measure the difference in sales.
